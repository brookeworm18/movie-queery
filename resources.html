Pratt Lib Guides: QFOC

 The Pratt Institute Library Guide for Queer Filmmakers of Color is a curated research page built by librarians to help you find films, articles, and databases in one place. It’s organized into clear sections like film resources, books, and external links, so you can move through it without guessing where to click. You start by selecting a topic, then follow links out to streaming platforms, catalogs, or academic sources. It’s especially useful for discovering queer films that are harder to find elsewhere. If you want something structured and intentional rather than algorithm-based, this is a strong starting point.

IMDB

 IMDb is a large, structured film database that includes detailed information on movies, shows, actors, and production credits. Its main features are search, film pages, and filtering tools. To explore queer cinema, you can search keywords like “LGBTQ” or follow actors and directors connected to queer films. Each film page links to related titles, making it easy to keep exploring. It’s not curated specifically for queer content, so it takes a bit more effort, but its depth makes it useful if you want detailed information or to trace connections across films.

Letterboxd

 Letterboxd is a social movie platform where you can track what you watch, read reviews, and explore lists made by other users. The main sections include your personal diary, reviews, and user-generated lists, which are key for discovery. To find queer cinema, you can search terms like “queer” or “LGBTQ+” and browse public lists that group films by theme or identity. Clicking into a film lets you jump to similar titles or see what others recommend. It’s easy to navigate and ideal if you prefer a casual, community-driven way to find new queer films.

BFI National Archive

 The BFI National Archive search tool is part of the British Film Institute’s collection, which preserves film and television history. The site is structured as a searchable catalog, where you can look up films, clips, and archival records. To find queer cinema, you can search specific terms or browse collections tied to identity, history, or genre. It’s less casual than other platforms, but it’s valuable for finding older, rare, or non-mainstream queer films. If you’re interested in film history or want to go beyond popular titles, this is a useful resource to explore.
